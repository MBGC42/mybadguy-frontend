Transparency

Data sources & methodology

Every score on MyBadGuy is derived from authoritative, publicly available data. We do not invent threat statistics. We do not exaggerate risk to drive engagement. This page documents exactly where our data comes from, how often it is updated, and how it feeds into your score.

📖
Factual only

Every claim is sourced to a government report, peer-reviewed research, or an authoritative security database. We do not use estimates or vendor marketing data.

🔄
Automatically updated

Live sources are ingested on automated schedules — not manually. Scores reflect the current state of the threat landscape, not a snapshot from a year ago.

🔎
Auditable

Every source listed here is publicly accessible. You can verify any statistic we cite by visiting the linked source directly. Nothing is behind a paywall.

⚖️
No shame, no exaggeration

Scores reflect your actual exposure profile — not worst-case fear. A lower score is genuinely lower risk. We do not inflate numbers to drive anxiety.

Live data sources

These sources are ingested automatically on a recurring schedule and directly influence your technical risk scores.

National Vulnerability Database (NVD)
National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) · U.S. Department of Commerce

The authoritative U.S. government repository of vulnerability management data. Every CVE (Common Vulnerability and Exposure) assigned by MITRE is enriched here with CVSS severity scores, affected version ranges, and configuration data. We query NVD for CVEs affecting iOS, iPadOS, Android, macOS, and Windows 11 and store them in our database.

342+ CVEs across 5 platforms
Updated every 2 hours via NVD REST API v2.0 · API key required (free)

Live · Daily
Known Exploited Vulnerabilities (KEV) Catalog
Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) · U.S. Department of Homeland Security

CISA's authoritative catalog of vulnerabilities that have been confirmed as actively exploited in the wild. A CVE appearing in the KEV catalog means it has been weaponized against real targets — not just theoretically possible. We cross-reference every CVE in our database against the KEV catalog daily and elevate scores accordingly. The KEV carries far more scoring weight than a CVE with no in-wild confirmation.

1,587 confirmed in-wild CVEs 52 affecting iOS/iPadOS
Updated daily via GitHub mirror (cisagov/kev-data) · CC0 public domain license
cisa.gov/kev →

The globally recognised knowledge base of adversary tactics, techniques, and procedures based on real-world observations. We ingest the Enterprise ATT&CK collection for named threat actor groups (173 groups, including nation-state APTs and cybercriminal organisations) and the Mobile ATT&CK collection for techniques targeting iOS and Android devices. Each group is mapped to our consumer actor categories and flagged if it is known to target individual consumers rather than enterprises.

Apple Security Advisories
Apple Inc.

Apple's official security release feed, published at support.apple.com. We parse individual advisory pages daily to extract CVE IDs, affected platforms (iOS, iPadOS, macOS), exploitation status, and point release version numbers. Apple's advisory language "Apple is aware of a report that this issue may have been exploited" is tracked as an exploitation signal — Apple typically leads CISA's KEV catalog by 48–72 hours on iOS exploits.

Microsoft Security Response Center (MSRC)
Microsoft Corporation

Microsoft's Patch Tuesday security bulletins published monthly. We ingest the current and prior month's bulletins via the MSRC CVRF REST API and extract Windows CVEs with their exploitability assessments — Exploitation Detected, Exploitation More Likely, or Exploitation Less Likely. CVEs marked "Exploitation Detected" set our in-wild flag and elevate Windows technical scores.

Fetched monthly · Second Tuesday of each month · No API key required

Live · Daily
OS Lifecycle Data
endoflife.date · MIT License

A community-maintained database of OS release cycles, support end dates, and latest version numbers for all five platforms we track. We use this to determine whether a detected OS version is current, behind, or end-of-life — a key input into patch status scoring. An end-of-life OS is treated as fully unpatched regardless of CVE counts, since no further security updates will be issued by the vendor.

Updated daily · Covers iOS, iPadOS, Android, macOS, and Windows

Scoring methodology

How your answers and device data combine into a score for each threat actor type.

Two independent scores per actor

Every threat actor produces two sub-scores — a Technical score and a Social score — which are averaged into a Combined score out of 99.

Technical score = base + consumer_boost + device_OS_boost + MDM_boost + location_boost Social score = base + consumer_boost Combined score = round((Technical + Social) / 2)

Technical scores are elevated by unpatched CVEs, MDM exposure, and public location usage. Social scores reflect how attractive your consumer profile is to each actor's psychological playbook — regardless of your device's patch status.

Device OS boost — powered by NVD + CISA KEV

Each unpatched CVE on your device platform contributes a weighted boost to technical scores. CVEs confirmed in the CISA KEV catalog — meaning actively exploited in the wild — contribute more than theoretical CVEs. Each actor type has a platform weighting that reflects how much they rely on technical exploits versus social engineering for that platform.

device_OS_boost = sum(unpatched_CVEs × actor_platform_weight × CVE_severity_factor) KEV CVEs count as confirmed exploitation — higher weight than theoretical CVEs

Consumer profile factors — 10 inputs

Each of the 10 profile questions maps to a weighted factor that is specific to each actor. An actor who targets the elderly weighs age heavily. A device thief weighs location and environment. An APT weights public profile and job data. The weighting for each factor is documented on each actor's detail page under the Weighted tab.

What we do not do

Transparency includes being clear about the limits of our model.

We do not scan your device

MyBadGuy does not access your files, contacts, photos, messages, or installed apps. Device detection is limited to what your browser reports — operating system type and version. Everything else is self-reported through the profile questions.

Scores are probabilistic, not diagnostic

A high score means your profile matches the characteristics that threat actors target — it does not mean you have been compromised or are under active attack. Scores reflect relative exposure, not confirmed incidents.

We do not use vendor threat intelligence

We do not pay for commercial threat intelligence feeds, vendor risk scores, or third-party data enrichment. Every data source is publicly available and government or open-source in origin. This prevents commercial conflicts of interest from influencing your score.

We are not a party to a third-party breach

We do not share your scan data with advertisers, data brokers, security vendors, or any third party. Your profile data — if you choose to save it — is stored encrypted in a Cloudflare database and is only accessible with your private ID and PIN. We cannot access it ourselves.

⚠️ Important limitation

MyBadGuy is an educational and awareness tool, not a professional security assessment. Scores are derived from population-level threat intelligence data and self-reported profile information. Individual circumstances may vary significantly. For a professional security assessment of your devices or organisation, consult a qualified cybersecurity professional.
