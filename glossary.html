Vulnerability terms

Common Vulnerabilities and ExposuresCVE

A unique identifier assigned to a publicly known security flaw in software or hardware. Each CVE has a number (e.g. CVE-2024-23225) and a description of what the flaw is and which products are affected. CVEs are the universal language of vulnerability tracking — every security tool, vendor advisory, and database uses the same IDs.

On MyBadGuy: We count how many CVEs affect your platform and OS version, and how many are confirmed to be actively exploited in the wild.
Common Vulnerability Scoring SystemCVSS

A 0–10 numerical score measuring the severity of a CVE. It considers how the vulnerability can be reached (network vs. physical access), how complex it is to exploit, what permissions are needed, and how much damage it can cause. A score of 9.0–10.0 is Critical; 7.0–8.9 is High; 4.0–6.9 is Medium; below 4.0 is Low.

On MyBadGuy: We use CVSS vector strings to determine which ATT&CK tactics a CVE enables.
Known Exploited VulnerabilityKEV

A CVE that CISA has confirmed is actively being exploited in real attacks against real targets — right now. Being on the KEV list is the strongest signal that a vulnerability is dangerous. CISA requires federal agencies to patch KEV vulnerabilities within deadlines; consumers should treat them as urgent.

On MyBadGuy: KEV status is a key input to your patch score. A KEV CVE on your platform significantly increases your risk score.
Exploit Prediction Scoring SystemEPSS

A machine learning model that estimates the probability (0.0–1.0) that a CVE will be exploited in the wild within the next 30 days. EPSS complements CVSS — a vulnerability can be low severity but have a high EPSS score because attackers have already built tools for it.

On MyBadGuy: We store EPSS scores alongside CVE data and weight high-probability vulnerabilities more heavily.
In the wild

A vulnerability is "in the wild" when there is confirmed evidence that attackers are actively using it in real attacks — not just in a research lab. In-wild CVEs are the ones that matter most because someone has already built the tool and is using it.

Patch

A software update that fixes a security vulnerability. When a vendor releases a patch, they are closing a door that attackers could walk through. Being "unpatched" means you are still running software with a known flaw that has not been fixed on your device yet.

End of lifeEOL

When a vendor stops releasing security updates for an OS version or device. No more patches means every vulnerability discovered after that date remains permanently unfixed. An EOL device is permanently vulnerable to any new attack discovered from that point forward.

On MyBadGuy: An EOL OS is treated as 100% vulnerable regardless of CVE count. EOL versions are flagged in red.
Zero-day0-day

A vulnerability that is exploited before the vendor knows about it — meaning there are zero days between discovery and attack. Zero-days are the most dangerous class of vulnerability because there is no patch available. Nation-state threat actors are the primary users of zero-days.

Indicator of CompromiseIOC

A piece of evidence that a device or network has been attacked or is communicating with an attacker's infrastructure. Common IOCs include IP addresses, domain names, and file hashes associated with known malware.

On MyBadGuy: We track active IOC counts from ThreatFox. High IOC counts boost Command & Control and Exfiltration tactic signals.

Threat actor terms

Threat actor

Any individual, group, or organization that poses a security threat. MyBadGuy tracks seven categories — each with different motivations, capabilities, and interest in your specific profile. Your scores reflect how attractive your profile is to each type, not whether you have been compromised.

Advanced Persistent ThreatAPT

A sophisticated, well-funded attacker — typically a nation-state or state-sponsored group — that conducts long-term, targeted intrusion campaigns. APTs are patient: they may spend months inside a target's systems before taking action. They use zero-days, custom malware, and supply chain attacks.

On MyBadGuy: Your APT score is driven primarily by your public profile, role, and device patch status. Journalists, executives, and government-adjacent individuals score highest.
Data broker

A company that collects, aggregates, and sells personal information about individuals — without their meaningful consent. Data brokers compile records from hundreds of sources: public records, loyalty programs, app tracking, social media, and purchased datasets.

On MyBadGuy: Data brokers are a passive threat — they do not hack you, they harvest you. Your data broker score is driven by social media use, public profile, and app permissions.
Stalkerware

Software secretly installed on a device to monitor someone's location, messages, calls, and activity — typically by an intimate partner or family member. Unlike legitimate parental controls, stalkerware hides itself from the device owner and sends data covertly. It is a tool of domestic abuse and coercive control.

Phishing

A social engineering attack where the attacker impersonates a trusted entity — a bank, a government agency, a tech company — to trick the victim into revealing credentials, clicking a malicious link, or transferring money. Phishing is the most common initial access method used by both cybercriminals and nation-state actors.

Ransomware

Malware that encrypts a victim's files and demands payment — typically in cryptocurrency — for the decryption key. Modern ransomware groups also steal data before encrypting it and threaten to publish it publicly if not paid. Ransomware is responsible for the majority of financial losses from cybercrime.

Mobile Device ManagementMDM

Software installed by an employer or institution that allows them to manage and monitor a device remotely. MDM can enforce security policies, wipe a device, install apps, and read certain device data. Having MDM on a personal device — or using a work device for personal activity — creates a dual exposure risk.

On MyBadGuy: MDM level is one of the 10 profile factors. A work-managed device significantly increases your insider threat and data broker scores.

ATT&CK tactics

MITRE ATT&CK is a globally recognized framework that categorizes the techniques attackers use at each stage of an attack. Each tactic represents a goal the attacker is trying to achieve. We use these tactics to link live CVE data to the most relevant security recommendations for your profile.

Initial AccessTA0001

How an attacker first gets into your device or account. Common methods include phishing emails, exploiting a vulnerability in a browser or app, and malicious downloads. This is the entry point — everything else follows from here.

ExecutionTA0002

Running malicious code on your device. Once an attacker has initial access, they need to execute code to do anything useful — install malware, steal data, or establish persistence.

PersistenceTA0003

Maintaining access to your device across reboots, password changes, and app restarts. Attackers use persistence techniques to ensure their malware survives device restarts and keeps running in the background without your knowledge.

Privilege EscalationTA0004

Gaining higher-level permissions than originally obtained. An attacker who gets in with limited access will try to escalate to administrator or root level — which removes most restrictions on what they can do.

Defense EvasionTA0005

Hiding from security tools and avoiding detection. Attackers disguise malware as legitimate apps, disable security features, and cover their tracks to stay on your device as long as possible without triggering alerts.

Credential AccessTA0006

Stealing usernames, passwords, authentication tokens, or biometric data. Credential theft allows attackers to impersonate you — logging into your bank, email, or cloud storage as if they were you. Stolen credentials are often sold and reused across multiple services.

CollectionTA0009

Harvesting data from your device before sending it to the attacker. This includes reading your messages, photos, contacts, location history, documents, and browser history. Collection happens silently in the background.

Command & ControlC2 · TA0011

Communication between malware on your device and the attacker's servers. C2 channels allow attackers to issue commands remotely — telling malware when to activate, what to steal, or when to encrypt your files.

ExfiltrationTA0010

Sending your data off your device to the attacker. Exfiltration is the point at which data theft is complete — your photos, documents, passwords, or financial records have left your device and are in the attacker's hands.

ImpactTA0040

Causing direct harm — deleting your files, encrypting your device for ransom, wiping your phone, or locking you out of your accounts. Ransomware is the most common consumer-facing impact tactic.

Frameworks & standards

MITRE ATT&CK

A globally recognized knowledge base of adversary tactics and techniques based on real-world observations. Maintained by MITRE Corporation and used by security teams worldwide to understand, detect, and defend against attacks. Free and publicly available at attack.mitre.org.

NIST Cybersecurity FrameworkNIST CSF 2.0

A voluntary framework published by the National Institute of Standards and Technology that provides guidance for managing cybersecurity risk. Organizes security activities into six functions: Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, and Recover. Many of our remediation recommendations are mapped to CSF 2.0 controls.

CIS ControlsCIS v8.1

A prioritized set of cybersecurity best practices published by the Center for Internet Security. IG1 — which we focus on — represents the minimum cybersecurity hygiene for any individual or small organization.

Multi-Factor AuthenticationMFA · 2FA

Requiring more than one form of verification to log in. The three factors are: something you know (password), something you have (phone, hardware key), and something you are (fingerprint, face). MFA means a stolen password alone is not enough to access your account.

On MyBadGuy: Enabling MFA on key accounts is a Critical-priority recommendation for almost every threat actor type.

Privacy & data terms

General Data Protection RegulationGDPR

The European Union's comprehensive data privacy law. GDPR gives individuals the right to access, correct, delete, and port their personal data. It applies to any organization that processes data about EU residents.

California Consumer Privacy ActCCPA · CPRA

California's consumer privacy law. Gives California residents the right to know what personal data is collected about them, to delete it, to opt out of its sale, and to not be discriminated against for exercising these rights.

Tracker

Code embedded in apps and websites that records your behavior — what you tap, how long you spend, where you go — and sends it to third parties for advertising and analytics. Most consumers have dozens of trackers running in apps they use daily.

Session storage

A browser storage mechanism that holds data only for the duration of a browser session — deleted automatically when you close the tab. MyBadGuy stores your detection profile in session storage so your data never reaches our servers unless you actively choose to save it.

Scoring terms

Technical score

A 0–99 score reflecting how vulnerable your device is to technical exploitation — based on your OS version, patch status, live CVE counts for your platform, device type, and work context. A higher technical score means your device has more unpatched vulnerabilities that the threat actor can exploit.

Social score

A 0–99 score reflecting how susceptible your profile is to social engineering and targeting — based on your age, financial situation, social isolation, social media use, and public profile.

Combined score

The average of your technical and social scores, rounded to the nearest whole number. Scores of 70–99 are High, 45–69 are Medium, and below 45 are Low. Each of the seven threat actors has their own combined score.

Patch scoreps

The number of unpatched CVEs on your platform at your OS version tier. This is a live count from our database, updated every two hours from NIST NVD. A patch score of 0 means you are fully current.

Tactic signal

A 0.0–1.0 weight representing how active a particular ATT&CK tactic is on your platform right now, based on live CVE data. Tactic signals are used to rank your recommendations — if Initial Access is highly active today, remediations that defend against it are surfaced first.

Organizations

CISA

The Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency — a U.S. federal agency under the Department of Homeland Security. CISA maintains the Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog and publishes cybersecurity advisories. cisa.gov

NIST

The National Institute of Standards and Technology — a U.S. federal agency that publishes cybersecurity standards and guidelines including the NVD, CSF 2.0, and NIST SP 800-63B. nist.gov

MITRE Corporation

A non-profit research organization that maintains the ATT&CK framework, the CVE program, and other critical security infrastructure. mitre.org

NVD

The National Vulnerability Database — the U.S. government repository of CVE data maintained by NIST. NVD enriches CVE records with CVSS scores and affected product data. It is the primary source of CVE data for MyBadGuy, updated every two hours. nvd.nist.gov

MSRC

The Microsoft Security Response Center — Microsoft's team responsible for investigating and responding to security vulnerabilities. MSRC publishes monthly security updates on Patch Tuesday with exploitability assessments for each CVE. msrc.microsoft.com

abuse.ch

A Swiss non-profit research project that tracks cybercrime infrastructure. Operates URLhaus (malware delivery URLs), MalwareBazaar (malware samples), and ThreatFox (threat intelligence IOCs) — all free, community-driven feeds. abuse.ch

FBI IC3

The Federal Bureau of Investigation's Internet Crime Complaint Center. IC3 collects and analyzes cybercrime complaints from the public and publishes an annual report. The IC3 2024 report is a primary source for our elder scammer and cybercriminal actor scoring. ic3.gov
